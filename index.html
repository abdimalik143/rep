Special about tarantulas:



Silent Ambush Predators

Unlike many other spiders, tarantulas do not rely on webs to catch their prey. Instead, they are ambush predators that rely on stealth and strength. They wait for prey to come close, then pounce quickly, using their strong legs and fangs to subdue it.

Long Lifespan

Tarantulas have an unusually long lifespan for spiders. Female tarantulas can live for 15 to 30 years, which is rare in the arachnid world. Males, however, tend to live only a few years after reaching maturity.

Unique Reproductive Behavior

Male tarantulas perform an elaborate courtship dance when they find a female. They tap and vibrate their legs to communicate, and after mating, males often die shortly thereafter.

Acanthoscurria geniculata (Brazilian White Knee)

Description:

A. geniculata is a New World terrestrial species found in Brazil, South America. This is a species that is above average size, with mild venom and urticating hairs. They can sometimes be defensive, but are relatively easier to deal with compared to other species. Like most New Worlds, they rarely bite and would usually kick urticating hairs as a defence mechanism, which can be irritating to the skin and a lot more harmful if it were to get into your eyes or if you breathe it in. Therefore, when handling this species, it is important to wear skin protection and to keep the spider away from your face. However, they are still considered an easy species to keep in terms of hardiness and overall interactions, as long as they are cared for and respected properly, there should be no real danger to both the keeper and spider. Hence, this species can be recommended to beginner keepers.

  • Locality: Brazil
  • Category: New Worlds

Harpactira pulchripes (Golden Blue Legged Baboon)

Description

H. pulchripes or the Golden Blue Legged Baboon is by far one of the more colourful tarantula species due to its vibrant golden colouration and striking blue legs, making it a very popular species in the tarantula keeping hobby. This is an Old World species and is found in the warm, arid scrublands of South Africa, hence they are a species that do not have urticating hairs BUT have more potent venom. Although, unlike most Old Worlds and ‘Baboon’ species, they tend to be less defensive and less prone to display threat poses or bite. Instead, they are more skittish and would prefer to hide when spooked. Nonetheless, it is important to note that this is still an Old World, which is not recommended for beginner keepers in general and it is HIGHLY discouraged to handle them. This species is classified as Fossorial, which means that they prefer to dig burrows and hide inside them most of the time. However, adults usually prefer hiding under structures like cork barks and such, webbing the surrounding area quite extensively so in a way, they sometimes behave similarly to terrestrial species. Additionally, this species has a medium growth rate, with the males maturing in around 1.5 years while females will take around 2 years to mature.

Fun Fact:Many people describe keeping Fossorial Species as having a ‘pet hole’ as the spider spends a majority of its time underground. This makes this species more unique and liked by many as they like to show themselves more. Of course, just don’t spook them or else they’ll immediately go into hiding.

  • Locality: South Africa
  • Category: Old Worlds

Davus Pentaloris (Guatemalan Tiger Rump)

Description:

This is a New World, terrestrial tarantula and an opportunistic burrower. This species comes from Southern Mexico and Guatemala which is a warm and temperate area with temperatures rarely going above 82f. Males of this species only live about 2.5-3 years while females have a lifespan of nearly 11 years. Females can grow to a size of 4-4.5in with most seeming to top out in size at 4”. The only sexual dimorphism in this species in size, so to determine the sex of your T you will need to examine a fresh molt or wait for the male to mature, emerging from its molt with tibial hooks and papal bulbs. This T should not to be confused with the Davus fasciatus which has a darker or black carapace, a different pattern on the abdomen, and originates from Costa Rica. Davus pentaloris is the only species firmly established in the hobby. It is sometimes mistakenly sold in the hobby as D. fasciatus. So if you think you own D. fasciatus, it is very likely a D. pentaloris. This species is known to be docile but can be skittish at times. Being a new world tarantula, this T does not have medically significant venom, but does have urticating hairs which can be very uncomfortable if the urticating setae gets on your skin and can cause serious problems if they were to get in your eyes, nose or mouth.

  • Locality: Southern Mexico and Guatemala
  • Category: New Worlds

Pterinochilus murinus (Orange Baboon)

P. murinus, also known as the Orange Baboon Tarantula, OBT or Orange Bitey Thing is one of the most notorious and well known species in the tarantula keeping hobby due to its bright and vibrant colours as well as its very bad attitude. This is an Old World species found around Central & South Africa, such as Angola, Burundi, Congo, Kenya, Tanzania, Zambia, and Zimbabwe just to name a few. As an Old World species, they do not have urticating hairs, but usually tend to have more potent venom and are known to bolt a lot more. Additionally, like most ‘baboon’ species, they can be extremely defensive, easily resorting to threat poses or bolting/running very fast. Granted, despite their bad reputation, they actually are not as bad as you may think and are not very dangerous to keep as most specimens are quite skittish and would choose to run and hide rather than to bite. Nonetheless, it is important to note that this is still an Old World, which is not recommended for beginner keepers in general and it is HIGHLY discouraged to handle them. Also, this is a terrestrial species but is classified as semi-arboreal as they are known to sometimes web quite extensively above ground. Hence, it is important to give the tarantula enough substrate AND climbing space for it as well as various anchor points for it to build webs on. Additionally, this species has a medium growth rate, with the males maturing in around 1.5 years while females will take around 2 years to mature.

Pterinopelma sazimai (Iridescent Blue)

Description:

Pterinopelma sazimai, also known as “Brazilian blue tarantula” or “Iridescent blue tarantula”, is a gorgeous NEW WORLD tarantula from the Bahia and Minas Gerais regions of Brazil. This is a very colorful blue tarantula that has been smuggled repeatedly to America and Europe and due to its shrinking habitat from deforestation, wildfire, and urban sprawl and farming, this tarantula is now endangered in its native location. Which makes it even more important to make sure you are getting your specimen from a reputable dealer that sells captive bred tarantulas. At first glance they don't look very blue and when they are in premolt, they look almost a grayish black. But right after a molt, you can see the blue hue with bright red setae that is even more apparent once you add a little direct light. This is a truly gorgeous specimen but they can be very skittish and a little defensive, so not the best choice for a beginner tarantula keeper.

The P. sazimai is found in the eastern part of Brazil and is in an environment that is very cool in the winter and very hot in the summer. They experience heavy rainfall as well as long periods of little moisture so they are very adaptable to their environment. They will usually burrow deep when they are looking to escape the heat or find more humidity, so you need to make sure you provide them with a nice depth of substrate. They are quick to throw a threat posture and kick hairs when they feel threatened and are very fast and will usually prefer to dive into their hide before showing any defensive behaviors.

  • Locality: East Brazil
  • Category: New Worlds
